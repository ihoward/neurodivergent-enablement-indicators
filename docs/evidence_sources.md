# Evidence Sources

This document defines approved source types for each evidence category. Evaluators must use sources consistent with these definitions when collecting evidence for NEI assessments.

---

## Inferred Evidence

Inferred evidence is collected from public sources without requiring organizational cooperation. These sources are observable by any researcher, journalist, or analyst.

### Approved Inferred Source Types

| Source Type | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Employee review platforms | Glassdoor, Blind, Indeed reviews | Aggregate signals, not individual anecdotes |
| Job descriptions | Public job postings | Reflect operational requirements and role design |
| Corporate sustainability reports | ESG reports, impact reports | May contain relevant policy disclosures |
| Annual reports | 10-K filings, shareholder letters | Governance and structural information |
| News coverage | Investigative journalism, business press | Use multiple sources; triangulate claims |
| Legal filings | EEOC charges, court records, NLRB filings | Document systemic patterns |
| NGO and association membership | Signatories to pledges, association rosters | Evidence of declared commitments |
| Regulatory filings | EEO-1 reports, pay equity disclosures | Structural workforce data |
| Conference and public speaking records | Presentations, panel participation | Evidence of organizational stance |

### Using Inferred Sources

- Inferred evidence describes what is **observable**, not what is certain.
- Evaluators should note the source type, date, and URL for each piece of inferred evidence.
- Single sources are generally insufficient. Look for convergent signals.
- Employee review data should be treated as aggregate signal. Individual reviews are not evidence.

---

## Declared Evidence

Declared evidence consists of public statements in which the organization asserts that a practice, policy, or arrangement exists. The organization is the source.

### Approved Declared Source Types

| Source Type | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Public policies | Employee handbook excerpts, published HR policies | Must be publicly accessible |
| Website statements | DEI pages, accommodations statements, benefits pages | Screenshot with date |
| Annual or sustainability reports | Policy disclosures in reports | Cross-reference with inferred sources |
| Press releases | Official announcements of programs or changes | Date of publication matters |
| Executive statements | Earnings call transcripts, public interviews | Attributable to specific spokesperson |
| Certifications and memberships | Disability:IN membership, B Corp certification | Verify currency |

### Using Declared Sources

- Declared evidence establishes that the organization has **stated** a practice exists.
- Declared evidence does not verify that the stated practice operates as described.
- Evaluators should record the date of the declaration. Policies change.
- Where a declared source conflicts with inferred evidence, both should be noted.

---

## Validated Evidence

Validated evidence is submitted by the organization to an accredited verifier who confirms that the evidence supports the indicator.

### Accredited Verifier Requirements

A verifier must:

- Be independent of the organization being evaluated
- Have documented verification procedures for the specific indicator
- Be accredited by a recognized body (list maintained separately)
- Issue a verification statement referencing the specific indicator ID and version

### Evidence Submitted for Validation

Organizations seeking validated status for an indicator may submit:

- Internal policy documents
- Operational data and records
- Third-party audits
- Employee feedback mechanisms and their outputs
- Process documentation

### Using Validated Evidence

- Validated evidence carries the highest evidentiary weight.
- A validation statement should reference the indicator ID, version, evidence category, and date of validation.
- Validations expire. Evaluate the currency of any validation statement.

---

## Source Reliability Hierarchy

When evidence categories conflict, or when evaluating the strength of evidence, use this hierarchy:

```
Validated  >  Declared  >  Inferred
```

However, **divergence** between categories is itself informative. An organization that declares a practice but where inferred evidence consistently contradicts that declaration is a meaningful finding.

---

## Prohibited Source Types

The following sources must not be used as evidence for NEI assessments:

| Source Type | Reason |
|---|---|
| Anonymous tips not corroborated by public sources | Not independently verifiable |
| Single employee accounts (identified or anonymous) | Not systematic evidence |
| Marketing or advertising copy | Self-promotional, not evidence of practice |
| Unverified social media posts | Authenticity and context cannot be confirmed |
| Internal documents obtained without authorization | Ethical and legal concerns |

---

## Documenting Sources

All evidence collected for an NEI assessment should be documented with:

- Source type (from the approved lists above)
- Source URL or citation
- Date accessed or published
- Evidence category (`inferred`, `declared`, or `validated`)
- The indicator ID and version the evidence supports
